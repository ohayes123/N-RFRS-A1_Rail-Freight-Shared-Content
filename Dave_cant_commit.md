# Dave Can't Commit

Dave has a knack for beginnings. He notices the little things first: a new café on the corner, a feature branch freshly forked in the morning, the bright arc of a promising conversation. He opens tabs, opens doors, opens pull requests. He signs up for plans and subscribes to hope. But when the calendar asks for a next step—an RSVP, a merged commit, a “Let’s make this official”—Dave shutters. The space between intending and committing becomes, for him, the whole country.

In relationships, Dave is charming and available in the ways that matter: he remembers birthdays, listens for hours, learns your favourite song. Yet when someone leans in with a question about “us,” he pivots like a branch waiting for a rebase. He’s frightened not by love itself but by permanence, by the weight of a promise that cannot be fast-forwarded or undone without consequence. He values freedom, potential futures that remain possible because nothing is pinned down.

On GitHub, the metaphor becomes literal. Dave clones a repository and starts to build—features, tests, good intentions. He crafts elegant commits with thoughtful messages, but the final step—`git push` and the nerve of `git commit` that sticks a change into history—feels terrifying. He imagines the wrong line of code becoming part of the story, the irreversible merge that might anger collaborators or reveal an imperfect choice. So he works locally, staging and unstaging, amending messages in a loop. His desktop is a museum of half-formed commits.

Both fears are related: the human dread of creating a recorded truth. A relationship commitment records a promise in the ledger of two lives; a Git commit records a decision in the ledger of a project. For Dave the ledger is sacred and frightening. He treats each entry as if future versions will judge it—rescue it if it was right, blame it if it was wrong. So he edits, delays, and defers in hope that more time will render the perfect commit.

Friends tell him that imperfect commitments still move life forward. Code can be reverted, branches can be corrected, and apologies—even bad ones—can be made. In the real world, too, hard, incomplete attempts teach what matters. Dave learns, slowly, to create small, low-stakes commitments: a short weekend away, a merge request on a draft branch, a promise to answer in twenty-four hours. Each small `git commit` and each modest relationship gesture is a rehearsal for larger trust.

The turning point is humble: Dave accepts that history isn’t only a place of judgment but also of conversation. He opens a pull request and labels it “WIP,” then invites feedback. He agrees to a monthly check-in rather than a lifetime contract. These choices let him test the waters without pretending to be perfect. Over time, the habit of committing—both to code and to people—becomes less like a verdict and more like a signal. It says, “I tried; I’m here; let’s iterate.”

Dave never becomes a portrait of certainty. He still hesitates; he still rewrites his messages. But he learns that the ledger of life and the ledger of code benefit from honest entries. Small commits lead to stronger features; small promises lead to stronger relationships. In the end, Dave learns to accept that mistakes can be fixed, merges can be rebased, and trust can be rebuilt. And with that acceptance, the act of committing loses some of its terror—and gains its purpose.

## The Great Commitment

It happens on a Tuesday, unremarkably sunny.

Dave sits at his desk, staring at a feature branch he's been nursing for three weeks. It's complete—truly complete. Tests pass, code is clean, documentation is written. His cursor hovers over the "Create Pull Request" button. The familiar panic rises: *What if there's a better way? What if someone finds a flaw? What if—*

But then his phone buzzes. It's Emma, the person he's been seeing for eight months. The text reads: "Coffee tomorrow? Want to talk about something."

His heart races. He knows what "want to talk about something" means. It means definition. Labels. Commitment. The conversation he's been gently postponing through clever topic changes and convenient distractions. He types three different responses and deletes them all.

Then something clicks.

Dave looks at his screen—the pull request button waiting patiently. He looks at his phone—Emma's message glowing softly. Two commitments, both terrifying, both necessary. Both... actually ready.

He thinks about all the small commits he's made. The weekend trips. The "WIP" pull requests that became production code. The promises to show up, which he kept. Each one a small act of bravery that didn't destroy him. Each one that made things better, not worse.

Dave takes a breath and clicks "Create Pull Request." His finger moves before his brain can object. Title: "Add user authentication feature." Description: honest, clear, complete. He hits submit.

The panic doesn't come. Instead, there's a strange lightness.

He picks up his phone and types: "Yes! Would love to talk. I've been thinking about us too. Tomorrow at 10?"

Send.

The world doesn't end. His computer doesn't explode. His phone doesn't burst into flames.

Instead, three things happen: his team lead sends a thumbs-up emoji on the PR, Emma replies with "Perfect ❤️," and Dave realizes he's smiling.

## Ever After

Six months later, Dave's GitHub profile shows a steady stream of merged pull requests. Not perfect commits—some needed revisions, some sparked debates, one accidentally broke the build and required a quick revert. But each one contributed. Each one moved the project forward.

Emma is now his girlfriend, officially, publicly, on social media and everything. They've had disagreements—about whose turn it is to choose the restaurant (Dave learned to just pick one), about communication styles, about the future. But they talk through it. They iterate. They commit to working on it.

Dave still feels the flutter of hesitation before big decisions. But now he recognizes it as a sign he cares, not a sign he should flee. He's learned that commitment isn't about being certain—it's about being willing to try, to adjust, to stay present even when things get complicated.

At work, he's known as reliable. His commits are frequent and honest. He labels things properly: "draft" when they're drafts, "ready for review" when they're ready. He's not afraid of the PR comments anymore; he sees them as collaboration, not judgment.

At home, Emma teases him about his "commit history"—the time he took forty-five minutes to choose a couch, the evening he rewrote a text message seven times before sending it. But she also knows that when Dave commits, he means it. When he says "I love you," it's not staged and waiting for approval—it's pushed, merged, and deployed.

One evening, they're making dinner together. Emma's chopping vegetables while Dave stirs a pot, and she says, casually, "I got offered a job in another city. Twelve-month contract."

Old Dave would have frozen. Would have found seventeen ways to avoid the conversation. Would have kept all options open until the decision made itself.

New Dave takes a breath, turns off the stove, and says, "Tell me about it. Let's figure this out together."

They talk for hours. About logistics and fears and what they both want. It's messy and uncertain and there's no perfect answer. But Dave doesn't run. He commits to the conversation, to the discomfort, to the work of building something with another person.

In the end, they decide together: Emma takes the job, Dave arranges to work remotely for part of the time, and they commit to making it work. Not because it's perfect or certain, but because it's worth it.

Later that night, Dave opens his laptop to finish a bug fix. He writes the code, tests it twice, and then—without hesitation—types:

```
git add .
git commit -m "Fix timezone bug in user sessions"
git push
```

No rewrites. No second-guessing. Just a simple, honest commit.

Emma looks over from the couch and smiles. "Nice commit message."

"Thanks," Dave says, closing his laptop. "I've been practicing."

He joins her on the couch, and they pick a movie—it only takes them three minutes to choose, which is practically a world record. As the opening credits roll, Dave realizes something profound: happiness isn't about making perfect choices or avoiding mistakes. It's about being willing to commit to the messy, imperfect, beautiful process of actually living.

Dave chose. He committed. He pushed. 

And he lived happily ever after—not because everything was perfect, but because he was finally, fully present for it.
