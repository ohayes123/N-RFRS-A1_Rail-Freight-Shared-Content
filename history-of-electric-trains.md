# The history of electric trains

## Introduction

Electric trains represent a pivotal chapter in the evolution of rail transport. Emerging from the limitations of steam technology in the latter half of the 19th century, electric traction offered cleaner, quieter, and more efficient alternatives for urban, suburban, and eventually long-distance rail services. Today, electrified railways form the backbone of high-speed and commuter rail networks across the globe. This article traces the origins, development, and enduring significance of electric railway technology.

## Origins of electric traction

### Early experiments

The theoretical basis for electric traction was established in the first half of the 19th century, following Michael Faraday's discoveries in electromagnetism in the 1820s and 1830s. The first practical demonstration of an electric railway is generally credited to Robert Davidson of Scotland, who built a small battery-powered locomotive in 1837. Though the technology was rudimentary, it proved the concept of applying electric power to rail vehicles.

The critical breakthrough came with the development of the dynamo, which enabled continuous generation of electrical current. In 1879, Werner von Siemens demonstrated a small electric railway at the Berlin Industrial Exposition. The locomotive ran on a third rail carrying 150 volts of direct current and hauled passengers around a 300-metre track. This demonstration attracted international attention and marked the beginning of practical electric railway technology.

### The first electric tramways

Urban tramways were the proving ground for electric traction. Horse-drawn trams had been common in city streets since the 1850s, but they were slow, expensive to operate, and dependent on animal welfare. Inventors and engineers in both Europe and North America began experimenting with electric replacements.

The first commercially successful electric tramway opened in Lichterfelde, near Berlin, in 1881, designed by Siemens and Halske. In Britain, an electric tramway opened in Blackpool in 1885, which remains operational today as a heritage tramway. The United States saw rapid adoption of electric tramways through the 1880s and 1890s, with Frank Sprague's Richmond, Virginia system of 1888 serving as a highly influential model.

## Development of electric railways

### Underground railways

The limitations of steam locomotives in enclosed spaces drove the development of electric traction for underground railways. The City and South London Railway, which opened in 1890 and ran beneath the streets of central London, was the first deep-level underground electric railway in the world. Its success demonstrated that electric traction was not merely suitable for short tramway applications but could power substantial railway operations.

London's underground network grew steadily over the following decades, with electrification gradually replacing steam traction on all lines. Other major cities including Paris, Budapest, New York, and Berlin developed their own underground electric railways during the same period.

### Mainline electrification

The transition from steam to electric traction on mainline railways was slower and more complex than for urban systems. Electrifying mainline routes required substantial investment in overhead wires or conductor rails, power generation infrastructure, and new rolling stock.

Early proponents of mainline electrification argued that electric locomotives were more powerful, more reliable, and cheaper to operate than steam equivalents on heavily trafficked routes. In the United States, the New York Central Railroad electrified its Manhattan approaches following a fatal accident caused by steam locomotive exhaust in 1902, and the Pennsylvania Railroad electrified substantial parts of its network through the early 20th century.

In Britain, mainline electrification proceeded more cautiously, with the Southern Railway leading electrification of suburban routes in the south of England through the 1920s and 1930s using a third-rail system at 660 volts direct current.

## Technological systems and standards

### Third rail versus overhead wire

Two competing systems emerged for supplying electricity to electric trains:

- third rail systems, in which a conductor rail alongside or between the running rails carries electrical current
- overhead wire systems (also known as catenary systems), in which a wire suspended above the track supplies current via a pantograph mounted on the vehicle

Third rail systems were simpler to install and were adopted for many underground railways and low-speed suburban networks. However, they present safety risks to persons on or near the track, and are unsuitable for high-speed operation. Overhead wire systems became the standard for high-speed and heavy rail applications worldwide.

### Direct current versus alternating current

Early electric railways used direct current (DC), which was well understood and readily available. However, DC systems suffer from voltage drop over long distances, requiring frequent substations to maintain adequate power supply.

Alternating current (AC) systems, operating at higher voltages, proved more efficient for long-distance power transmission. The development of AC electric railways from the early 20th century onward allowed electrified networks to expand without excessive infrastructure costs. France, Germany, and Switzerland standardized on AC systems for their mainline railways, while Britain retained a mix of DC and AC systems that persists to the present day.

## High-speed electric railways

### Origins of high-speed rail

The most significant achievement of electric railway technology has been the development of high-speed rail. Early demonstrations of high-speed electric operation occurred in the early 20th century, when German and French railways set speed records exceeding 200 kilometres per hour on specially prepared tracks.

### The Shinkansen

The modern era of high-speed rail began in Japan with the opening of the Tōkaidō Shinkansen in 1964. The Shinkansen, popularly known as the "bullet train", connected Tokyo and Osaka at speeds of up to 210 kilometres per hour. Japan's Shinkansen network expanded steadily over subsequent decades, and the technology was refined to achieve and sustain commercial speeds exceeding 300 kilometres per hour.

The Shinkansen demonstrated that electric railways could offer a compelling alternative to air travel for intercity journeys of up to around 800 kilometres, combining high speed with high frequency, punctuality, and safety.

### European high-speed networks

Europe followed Japan's example in developing high-speed electric railways. France's Train à Grande Vitesse (TGV), launched in 1981 between Paris and Lyon, achieved commercial speeds of 260 kilometres per hour and later set the world speed record for conventional wheeled rail vehicles, reaching 574.8 kilometres per hour on a test run in 2007.

Germany's InterCityExpress (ICE) network followed in 1991, connecting major cities at speeds up to 300 kilometres per hour. Spain, Italy, the Netherlands, and other European countries subsequently developed their own high-speed networks, many of which are interconnected across borders.

In Britain, the High Speed 1 line opened in 2007, connecting London's St Pancras International station to the Channel Tunnel and enabling direct Eurostar services to Paris and Brussels at speeds up to 300 kilometres per hour.

## Electric trains in urban and suburban transport

### Suburban and commuter rail

Electric traction transformed suburban and commuter rail services. Rapid acceleration, high frequency of service, and clean operation made electric trains ideally suited to high-density urban environments. Electrified suburban networks grew around major cities throughout the 20th century, enabling the expansion of suburbs and reducing urban congestion.

London's suburban network became one of the most extensive electrified commuter rail systems in the world, handling millions of passenger journeys each day. Similar dense electrified networks developed around Paris, Tokyo, New York, Mumbai, and other major metropolitan areas.

### Light rail and tram revival

From the 1970s onward, many cities that had previously abandoned their tram systems began to reintroduce light rail as a sustainable urban transport solution. Modern light rail vehicles are sophisticated electric trains, offering low-floor access, high passenger capacity, and low emissions compared with diesel buses.

In Britain, new light rail systems opened in Manchester (1992), Sheffield, Nottingham, Birmingham, and Edinburgh, reflecting a wider revival of interest in electric urban transport as concerns about congestion, air quality, and carbon emissions grew.

## Environmental and economic advantages

Electric trains offer a range of advantages over their fossil-fuel-powered counterparts:

- significantly lower carbon emissions when powered from renewable or low-carbon electricity sources
- reduced air and noise pollution, particularly in urban areas
- lower energy consumption per passenger kilometre than road or air transport
- lower maintenance costs resulting from fewer moving parts and regenerative braking, which returns energy to the network
- superior performance on gradients and at high speeds

These advantages have driven increasing investment in railway electrification as governments worldwide seek to decarbonize their transport sectors.

## Contemporary developments

### Maglev technology

Magnetic levitation (maglev) technology represents the most advanced expression of electric railway engineering. Maglev trains use electromagnetic force to levitate the vehicle above the track, eliminating wheel-rail friction and enabling extremely high speeds. Japan's SCMaglev system has achieved test speeds of 603 kilometres per hour, the world record for any guided ground vehicle.

Maglev systems operating commercially include Japan's SCMaglev Chūō Shinkansen corridor (under construction for opening in 2027) and the Shanghai Transrapid in China, which operates at up to 430 kilometres per hour.

### Battery and hydrogen electric trains

Where overhead electrification infrastructure is absent, battery-electric and hydrogen fuel cell trains offer alternatives to diesel traction that avoid the cost of electrification while retaining the operational and environmental benefits of electric drive systems. Germany introduced the world's first hydrogen passenger train service in 2018; Britain has also conducted trials of battery and hydrogen-electric traction on unelectrified routes.

## Legacy and outlook

Electric railways have fundamentally transformed urban mobility, enabled intercity high-speed travel, and are increasingly positioned as central components of national decarbonization strategies. From Robert Davidson's modest battery locomotive in 1837 to the SCMaglev setting records at 603 kilometres per hour, electric railway technology has evolved continuously in response to changing social, economic, and environmental demands.

As investment in rail electrification accelerates worldwide, the electric train looks set to remain at the heart of sustainable transport systems for the foreseeable future.
