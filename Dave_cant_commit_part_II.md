# Dave Can't Commit (Part II)

Dave stared at his screen, his cursor blinking mockingly in the terminal window. `git commit -m "..."` he typed, then immediately hit backspace until the message was gone. What if this wasn't the right commit message? What if he should split this into two commits? Three? Maybe he should squash everything and rewrite the entire branch history first?

"Dave, you coming to lunch?" Sarah called from across the office.

"Uh, yeah, just need to... commit this change," he said, not looking up.

"You said that twenty minutes ago," she laughed.

It was true. Dave had been hovering over the commit button since 11:47. It was now 12:17. The change was simple—he'd fixed a typo in a comment. Three characters. But what if someone had already fixed it on another branch? What if this commit message format didn't match the team's convention? Did they use present tense or past tense? Sentence case or lowercase?

Finally, Sarah dragged him away from his desk. "Come on, before everywhere gets crowded."

They stood outside the office building, surrounded by food trucks and cafes. Dave's stomach rumbled, but his mind raced faster.

"So, what are you thinking?" Sarah asked, already walking toward the Thai place.

"Well..." Dave stopped in his tracks. "I mean, Thai is good, but what if I want something lighter? Or maybe heavier? What if I get Pad Thai and then regret not getting the curry? But if I get curry, what kind? Red or green? Actually, what if—"

"Dave."

"—we should go to that new sandwich place? But they have 47 types of bread. How do I choose bread, Sarah? HOW?"

Sarah checked her watch. "You have 5 minutes before I'm eating without you."

Dave's phone buzzed. A Slack message from his team lead: "Dave, can you please just commit that typo fix so I can merge the PR?"

He looked at his phone, then at the food trucks, then back at his phone. Two types of commitment, both impossible. He opened his GitHub app and stared at the staged changes. He looked up at the menu boards, each one a labyrinth of choices.

"You know what?" Sarah said, grabbing his arm. "I'm ordering for you. You're getting Pad Thai. And when we get back, you're committing that code with the message 'fix typo' because that's literally what it is."

"But—"

"No buts. Sometimes you just have to commit, Dave."

Dave followed her to the Thai truck, feeling both relieved and anxious. As they waited in line, he pulled out his phone and quickly typed `git commit -m "fix typo"` and hit enter before he could overthink it. There. Done. The world didn't end.

His phone immediately buzzed with approvals. "FINALLY," his team lead wrote. "PR merged. Good job."

"See?" Sarah said, handing him his Pad Thai. "Commitment isn't so scary."

Dave took a bite. It was delicious. Perfect, even. Then a terrible thought struck him: "Should I have gotten the spring rolls too?"

Sarah just shook her head and laughed.

---

*Some say Dave is still standing in front of food trucks to this day, trapped in an endless loop of "what ifs" and uncommitted changes. But every once in a while, with a gentle push from friends and increasingly urgent Slack messages, he manages to click that button and make a choice. And sometimes, just sometimes, it turns out perfectly fine.*
